### Point of View: Moral Wrongness

In simple terms, ***Erich Hatala Matthes's*** "oppression account" provides a specific reason **why** cultural appropriation is **morally wrong**.

His argument is that the act of "taking" (appropriation) is not just a neutral exchange. Because it happens within a dynamic of power imbalance, the taking itself **actively increases the inequality and marginalization** that the less powerful group already suffers from.

So, for Matthes, the moral wrongness isn't just about hurt feelings or ownership; it's wrong because it is an act that **contributes to and reinforces systemic oppression**.

### Commodification

In simple terms, **commodification is the process of turning a cultural good or idea into an object of trade**‚Äîsomething that can be bought and sold.

When this happens, the item's original value is stripped away. Its social history, the conditions of its creation, and its deep cultural meaning are all masked and mystified.

In my paper, I explain this using a classic distinction:

- **Use Value:** What the item *is* and *means* within its culture (e.g., a sacred symbol).
- **Exchange Value:** What the item is *worth* commercially when it's sold as a product (e.g., a $20 t-shirt printed with that symbol).

Cultural appropriation is driven by this switch. The core issue is that **not everything is or should be for sale**.

### The "Spice" Analogy and Double Standards

A common practice is when a dominant culture takes elements from a marginalized culture purely for **aesthetic reasons**.

I use the writer bell hooks's powerful quote to describe this. She says "ethnicity becomes spice, seasoning that can liven up the dull dish that is mainstream white culture".

This leads to a harmful **double standard** I mentioned earlier:

- A person from a minority group is **ostracized** or stereotyped for the visible markers of their culture.
- When a white person adopts those *same markers*, it's suddenly seen as "exotic, desirable, and edgy".

This process **inverts the meaning**. The object, which might be a political symbol of oppression for the marginalized group, is **trivialized** and distorted into a simple fashion accessory.

### Pizza-Effect / re-enculturation

The "pizza-effect" (re-enculturation) is a specific *example* of the harmful commodification.

The dominant culture **sells the new, re-packaged version back to the original culture**.

Because the dominant culture has so much influence (what my paper calls "**cultural imperialism**"), this new version is suddenly seen as more valuable, more prestigious, or more legitimate than the original.

The original culture then has to **"re-learn" or "re-enculturate" its own item** in this new, altered form.

---

### üçï The Pizza Example

This is exactly what happened with pizza:

1. **Original Item:** In Naples, it was a "simple food stuff"‚Äîjust a hot flatbread with herbs.
2. **Taken & Changed:** Italian immigrants took it to the USA and transformed it with elaborate toppings and different sizes (creating what we now know as "pizza Americana").
3. **Sold Back:** This new, American-style pizza was then **exported back to Italy** after WWI.
4. **The Effect:** This "global identity" version became hugely popular in Italy, gaining a status the original, simple flatbread never had.

So, the "pizza-effect" is when a dominant culture acts like a **global trendsetter**, taking something from a marginalized culture, changing it, and selling it back to them as the "new and improved" version, which reinforces the dominant culture's power and distorts the original.

## Some Ethical Challenges

The course highlights four major ethical challenges:

### 1. Scale Challenge
- Technologies and systems often affect **large numbers of people**
- Small design decisions can have **massive societal impact**

### 2. Power Challenge
- Designers, organizations, and institutions often hold **asymmetric power**
- Decisions made by a few can strongly influence the lives of many

### 3. Vulnerability Challenge
- Certain individuals or groups are **especially vulnerable**
- Ethics must account for protection, care, and responsibility toward those at risk

### 4. Democracy Challenge
- Technology can influence:
  - Political participation
  - Public discourse
  - Democratic decision-making
- Ethical reflection is needed to safeguard democratic values

## Philosophy 101

**Philosophy** is the systematic reflection on fundamental questions about our lives and our environment.

### Example Questions Addressed by Philosophy
- Why do we live?
- What is our purpose as humans?
- How are body and mind connected?
- Are we free or determined?
- What is morally right and wrong?
- What is just or fair?

### Key Characteristics
- Often deals with:
  - Truth
  - Knowledge
  - Morality
  - Justice
- Rarely provides clear, final answers
- Focuses on thinking, knowledge, and responsibility

### Main Areas of Philosophy
- Logic
- Epistemology
- Ethics

## Ethics 101

**Ethics** is a field of philosophy.

### What Ethics Is About
- Metaethics vs. normative ethics
- A theory of right behavior
- Developing criteria for moral judgment
- Systematizing moral beliefs
- Providing orientation for decision-making and action

> **Ethics asks:** *What should I do?*

### Basis of Ethics
- Moral beliefs:
  - What is good or bad?
  - What is fair or unfair?

### Important Note
- There is **no single ethics**
- Different interpretations of good, bad, fair, and unfair exist

---

## Normativity

**Normativity** tells us what we **should** do.

### Norms
- Unwritten laws in societies
- Describe actions or outcomes as:
  - Good and desirable
  - Bad and undesirable
- Specify how we should act
- Provide reasons and justifications for:
  - Actions
  - Beliefs

### Philosophy and Normativity
- Ethics is the philosophical field that primarily deals with normativity

---

## Normative vs. Descriptive Statements

### Normative Statements
- Say how things **should be**
- Contain value judgments
- Formulate rules

### Descriptive Statements
- Say how things **are**
- Describe reality
- Are value-free
- Can be empirically verified

---

## Morals

**Morals** are:
- Values and rules (norms)
- Shared by a society or group
- Generally accepted

### Moral Action
- Saying “Person X acted morally” means:
  - Their behavior is considered right and good by society

### Ethics and Morals
- Ethics reflects on and examines moral values and rules

---

## Agency

**Agency** is the ability to act and take responsibility.

### Key Concepts
- An **agent** is an entity that can act
- Action theory:
  - Actions are caused by mental states
  - Examples: intentions, desires, deliberations

### Agency Refers To
- Exercising the capacity to act

### Philosophy of Action
- Standard concept of action:
  - Action is understood in terms of **intentionality**

---

## Autonomy

**Autonomy** is the ability to act in a self-determined way.

### Individual Autonomy
- The ability to live one’s life in a self-determined manner
- Acting according to one’s own:
  - Reasons
  - Motives

### Key Aspects
- Not acting due to manipulative external influences
- Autonomy is a **central value in philosophy**
